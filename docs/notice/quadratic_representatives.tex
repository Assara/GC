\begin{abstract}
For every odd $N \ge 5$, we prove that, in Kontsevich's graph complex equipped with the edge-contraction differential, the wheel graph $W_N$ is homologous to an explicit linear combination of $2^{(N-3)/2}$ graphs, each having only $3$- and $4$-valent vertices. Combined with the Khoroshkin--Merkulov construction of the action of the even graph complex on finite-dimensional quadratic Poisson structures \cite{KhoroshkinMerkulov2023}, this implies that the odd wheel classes act non-trivially on such structures.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
By the results of Khoroshkin and Merkulov on deformation quantization of quadratic Poisson structures \cite{KhoroshkinMerkulov2023}, the even graph complex acts on finite-dimensional quadratic Poisson structures.
However, the faithfulness of this action is not established there.
More precisely, a cohomology class acts non-trivially on quadratic Poisson structures if and only if it does not vanish under the projection
\begin{equation}\label{eq:projection-p}
p \colon H(\GC_2,\delta)\to H\bigl(\GC_2/\langle \GC_2 \rangle_{\ge 5},\delta\bigr).
\end{equation}
Here $\langle \GC_2 \rangle_{\ge 5}$ denotes the differential closure of the subspace spanned by graphs having a vertex of valence at least $5$.
Thus, to prove nontriviality of a class, it suffices to show that it does not vanish under the projection \eqref{eq:projection-p}.
While it is conjectured that the projection \eqref{eq:projection-p} is a quasi-isomorphism, very few classes have been shown not to vanish under it.
In this paper we consider the odd wheel classes
\[
\mathfrak{w}_N =
\begin{tikzpicture}[baseline=-0.05cm,scale=0.42]
  \tikzset{wheelvertex/.style={circle,fill=black,draw=black,inner sep=0pt,minimum size=2.2pt}}
  \draw[line width=0.4pt] (0,0) circle (1.25);
  \draw[line width=1.2pt,white] (45:1.25) arc[start angle=45,end angle=135,radius=1.25];
  \node[wheelvertex] (c) at (0,0) {};
  \foreach \a in {165,225,285,345,15} {
    \node[wheelvertex] (v\a) at (\a:1.25) {};
    \draw[line width=0.4pt] (c) -- (v\a);
  }
  \fill (-0.18,1.55) circle (0.05);
  \fill (0,1.55) circle (0.05);
  \fill (0.18,1.55) circle (0.05);
\end{tikzpicture}
\;+\; \cdots,
\]
and show that they remain nonzero under the projection \eqref{eq:projection-p}, and thus act non-trivially on finite-dimensional quadratic Poisson structures.

We prove this by constructing explicit representatives of the corresponding homology classes in the (pre)dual graph complex, involving only graphs whose vertices have valence $3$ or $4$.

The odd wheel classes $[\mathfrak{w}_N]$ occupy a distinguished place in the graph complex.
By Willwacher's identification of the degree-zero cohomology of Kontsevich's graph complex with the Grothendieck--Teichm\"uller Lie algebra \cite{Willwacher2015}, they correspond to the conjectural generators of $\mathfrak{grt}_1$ arising in the Deligne--Ihara conjecture. Brown showed that the free Lie algebra generated by the odd generators embeds into $\mathfrak{grt}_1$ \cite{Brown2012}.

\section{Reformulation to the Dual Perspective}
Instead of working with the vertex-splitting differential $\delta$, we shall work with the edge-contraction differential $d$.
From this (pre)dual perspective, the cohomological statement above becomes a homological statement about low-valence representatives.

\begin{conjecture}
Let $\widetilde{(\GC_d)_{<5}}$ be the maximal subcomplex of $(\GC_d,d)$ spanned by linear combinations of graphs all of whose vertices have valence $3$ or $4$.
Then the inclusion
\[
i \colon \bigl(\widetilde{(\GC_d)_{<5}},d\bigr)\hookrightarrow (\GC_d,d)
\]
is a quasi-isomorphism.
\end{conjecture}

With the edge-contraction differential, the homology classes corresponding to the $\mathfrak{grt}$ generators may be represented by single wheel graphs $W_N$:
\[
W_N =
\begin{tikzpicture}[baseline=-0.05cm,scale=0.42]
  \tikzset{wheelvertex/.style={circle,fill=black,draw=black,inner sep=0pt,minimum size=2.2pt}}
  \draw[line width=0.4pt] (0,0) circle (1.25);
  \draw[line width=1.2pt,white] (45:1.25) arc[start angle=45,end angle=135,radius=1.25];
  \node[wheelvertex] (c) at (0,0) {};
  \foreach \a in {165,225,285,345,15} {
    \node[wheelvertex] (v\a) at (\a:1.25) {};
    \draw[line width=0.4pt] (c) -- (v\a);
  }
  \fill (-0.18,1.55) circle (0.05);
  \fill (0,1.55) circle (0.05);
  \fill (0.18,1.55) circle (0.05);
\end{tikzpicture}.
\]
However, for $N>3$, the wheel graph $W_N$ does not lie in the subcomplex $\widetilde{(\GC_d)_{<5}}$.
In this paper we prove the following theorem.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:main-reduction}
For $d$ even and each odd $N \ge 5$, there exists an element $U_N \in \GC_d$ such that
\[
W_N-d(U_N)\in \widetilde{(\GC_d)_{<5}}.
\]
\end{theorem}

Theorem~\ref{thm:main-reduction} is the homological statement dual to the nonvanishing of $p([\mathfrak{w}_N])$.

\section{Conventions}
We shall assume familiarity with Kontsevich's graph complex in this paper; for a formal definition and further discussion, see \cite{MerkulovSurvey}.
To fix conventions, we will consider $\GC_d$ to consist of connected graphs in which every vertex is at least $3$-valent.

We will use vertex and edge labels starting from $0$.
We will exclusively consider the case that $d$ is even. In this case, vertex labels and edge directions do not matter, and edge labels matter only through the sign orientation.

The differential $d$ is the edge-contraction differential. If a graph has $m$ edges, then the contraction of the edge labelled $i$ carries the sign
\[
(-1)^{i+m+1}.
\]
After contracting the edge labelled $i$, we relabel the remaining edges by preserving their relative order: labels smaller than $i$ remain unchanged, while each label greater than $i$ is decreased by $1$.


\section{Two Families of Graphs}
The proof is organized around two explicit families of graphs.
The family $V_N(s)$ describes the quadratic support that remains after eliminating the higher-valence part of the wheel class, while the family $U_N(s)$ provides the auxiliary chains whose differentials perform this elimination.
The construction of $V_N(s)$ may be summarized as follows.

\medskip
\noindent
\textbf{Construction of $V_N(s)$ and $U_N(s)$.}
\begin{enumerate}[itemsep=0.35em, topsep=0.35em]
\item Input: an odd integer $N=2m+1$ and a binary sequence $s=(s_1,\dots,s_k)$ of choices, with $s_i \in \{\LeftChoice, \RightChoice\}$. 

\item \label{it:seed} Initialize the graph with edges
\[
(0,1),\ (0,2),\ (0,3),\ (1,2),\ (2,3). 
\]
Denote vertex $1$ L, vertex $2$ C and vertex $3$ R.

\item \label{it:iterate} For $i=1,\dots,k$:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*), itemsep=0.25em, topsep=0.25em]
\item \label{it:spine} Add a new vertex attached to the current centre vertex $C$, and denote the new vertex by $\texttt{new\_C}$. The new edge $(\texttt{C},\texttt{new\_C})$ gets the label $4i+1$. 

\item \label{it:triangle1} Add an edge $(\texttt{new\_C},R)$ (if $s_i=\LeftChoice$, we instead add the edge $(\texttt{new\_C},L)$). This edge gets the label $4i+2$.
\item \label{it:triangle2} Add a second new vertex denoted $\texttt{new\_R}$ (or $\texttt{new\_L}$). Add an edge $(\texttt{R},\texttt{new\_R})$ (or $(\texttt{L},\texttt{new\_L})$). This new edge gets the label $4i+3$.
\item \label{it:triangle3} Add an edge $(\texttt{new\_C},\texttt{new\_R})$ (or $(\texttt{new\_C},\texttt{new\_L})$). This edge gets the label $4i+4$.
\item Denote the vertices $\texttt{new\_C}$ and $\texttt{new\_R}$ (or $\texttt{new\_L}$) by $C$ and $R$ (or $L$) respectively. 

\end{enumerate}

After these $k$ steps, the graph has $2k+4$ vertices and $4k+5$ edges.  

\item \label{it:U}(Only for $U_N(S)$) 
Add one further centre vertex $\texttt{new\_C}$ and an edge $(\texttt{C},\texttt{new\_C})$. This edge gets the label $4k+5$.
Denote the new vertex $C$.

\item \label{it:arc} 
Now, for both $V_N(S)$ and $U_N(S)$, let $m = N-2k-3$ be the number of remaining vertices to be added, and let $n$ denote the current largest vertex label.
If $m = 0$, add the final edge $(L,R)$, with edge label $2N-1$ (This case will only be considered for $V_N(S)$). 
Otherwise, add the remaining vertices and edges as an arc of triangles as follows: 
Let us denote the least unassigned edge label $e = 4k+5$ for $V_N(S)$ and $e = 4k+6$ for $U_N(S)$.

\begin{enumerate}
\item \label{it:left_arc} Add $(L, n+1)$ with edge label $e$.
\item \label{it:mid_arc} For $i = 1, \ldots, m-1$: Add $(n+i, C)$ with edge label  $e+ 2i-1$, and $(n+i, n+i+1)$ with edge label $e+ 2i$.
\item \label{it:right_arc} Add $(n+m, R)$ with edge label $2N-1 $ for $V_N(S)$ and $2N+1$ for $U_N(S)$. 

\end{enumerate}
\end{enumerate}

We now record some simple structural properties of the graphs constructed above.

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:VN-properties}
Let $S^{\mathrm{op}}$ be the binary sequence obtained from $S$ by interchanging $\LeftChoice$ and $\RightChoice$ in every entry.
Then the following statements hold.
\begin{enumerate}[itemsep=0.35em, topsep=0.35em]
\item \label{it:lr_iso} The graphs $V_N(S)$ and $V_N(S^{\mathrm{op}})$ are isomorphic. 
Likewise, $U_N(S)$ and $U_N(S^{\mathrm{op}})$ are isomorphic. 
If N is odd, then the isomorphism has an even edge permutation.

\item \label{it:V_is_wheel} The graph $V_N(\emptyset)$ is the wheel graph $W_N$.

\item \label{it:V_low_valence} For a binary sequence $S$ of length $(N-3)/2$, every vertex of $V_N(S)$ has valence at most $4$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}


\begin{proof}


For item~\ref{it:lr_iso}, the isomorphism is given by reversing the order in which we add the edges in Steps~\ref{it:seed} and \ref{it:arc}. Keep in mind that a permutation reversing the order of $n$ items is even if $n \equiv 0,1 \pmod{4}$ and odd otherwise.
In Step~\ref{it:seed}, we add $5$ edges, which reverses with two vertex transpositions.
In Step~\ref{it:arc}, we add $2N-4k-5$ edges. For $N=2m+1$, we have
\[
2N-4k-5 = 4m-4k-3 \equiv 1 \pmod{4},
\]
so this reversal is even.
 
For $V_N(\emptyset)$, we skip from Step \ref{it:seed} directly to Step \ref{it:arc}. This results in a wheel graph where vertex $2$ is the center hub vertex. 

Finally, we note that for any $V_N(S)$, the only vertex which may be more than $4$-valent is the vertex denoted C in Step \ref{it:arc}.
For a sequence of length $(N-3)/2$, we do not have any extra edges to attch to this vertex. 


\end{proof}

\FloatBarrier

\section[Differential of the family U\_N(s)]{Differential of \texorpdfstring{$U_N(s)$}{U_N(s)}}

Let us now consider the edge contraction differential on $U_N(s)$. 

The aim of this section is to prove the following Lemma.
\begin{lemma}\label{lem:differential-UN}
Assume that $N$ is odd. Let $\mathcal{S}_k$ denote the set of all binary sequences of choises $\{\LeftChoice, \RightChoice\}$ of length $k$. Then
\[
 d \left(\sum_{S \in \mathcal{S}_k} U_N(S) \right)
 = \sum_{T \in \mathcal{S}_{k+1}} V_N(T) - \sum_{S \in \mathcal{S}_k} V_N(S).
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
For each $U_N(S)$ there are only a handful of edges that may be contracted without creating a double edge, which would kill the resulting graph in the even graph complex.

The edges that may be contracted are the following.
\begin{enumerate}[itemsep=0.35em, topsep=0.35em]
\item The edge labelled $4k+5$, created in Step~\ref{it:U}. The resulting graph is identical to $V_N(S)$. The sign of this contraction is
\[
(-1)^{4k+5+2N+1+1} = -1.
\]

\item The edge labelled $4k+6$, created in Step~\ref{it:left_arc}. This creates a graph that is identical to $V_N(S,\LeftChoice)$. The sign of this contraction is $+1$.

\item The edge labelled $2N+1$, created in Step~\ref{it:right_arc}. This creates a graph that is isomorphic to $V_N(S,\RightChoice)$. The sign of this contraction is $+1$.

The isomorphism is given by swapping the edges labelled $2N-1$ and $2N-3$ and by the three insertions
\[
2N-3 \mapsto 4(k+1)+1,
\]
\[
2N-2 \mapsto 4(k+1)+2,
\]
\[
2N-1 \mapsto 4(k+1)+3.
\]
All these four permutations carry odd sign, so the composed sign is even.

\item Finally, some edges $4i+1$, for $i=2,\dots,k$, created in Step~\ref{it:spine}, may be contracted without creating a double edge. Let us denote such a graph by $U_N^i(S)$.
\end{enumerate}

Consider
\[
U_N^i(s_1,\dots,s_{i-1},s_i,\dots,s_k)
\]
for a sequence $S=(s_1,\dots,s_k)$. If $s_i=s_{i-1}$, then $U_N^i(S)$ vanishes because it contains a double edge.

Otherwise, the graphs
\[
U_N^i(s_1,\dots,s_{i-2},\LeftChoice,\RightChoice,\dots,s_k)
\]
and
\[
U_N^i(s_1,\dots,s_{i-2},\RightChoice,\LeftChoice,\dots,s_k)
\]
are isomorphic, where the isomorphism is given by the three edge transpositions coming from \ref{it:triangle1}, \ref{it:triangle2}, and \ref{it:triangle3} of Step~\ref{it:iterate} in the construction list above. Therefore,
\[
U_N^i(s_1,\dots,s_{i-1},s_i,\dots,s_k)
=
-U_N^i(s_1,\dots,s_{i-2},s_i,s_{i-1},s_{i+1},\dots,s_k).
\]
Hence these terms cancel pairwise in the sum over all sequences. It follows that
\[
\begin{aligned}
\sum_{S \in \mathcal{S}_k} d\bigl(U_N(S)\bigr)
&= \sum_{S \in \mathcal{S}_k} \Bigl( V_N(S,\LeftChoice) + V_N(S,\RightChoice) - V_N(S) \Bigr)\\
&= \sum_{T \in \mathcal{S}_{k+1}} V_N(T) - \sum_{S \in \mathcal{S}_k} V_N(S).
\end{aligned}
\]

\end{proof}

\section{Proof of the Main Theorem}
It is now time to prove Theorem~\ref{thm:main-reduction}. Let us first make a more precise statement, in which we define $U$.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:explicit-representatives}
For $k\geq 1$, let $\mathcal{S}_k^{\LeftChoice}\subset \mathcal{S}_k$ denote the subset of binary sequences of length $k$ whose first entry is $\LeftChoice$.
For each odd $N\geq 5$, define
\[
U_N^{(0)}:=U_N(\emptyset),
\]
and
\[
U_N^{(k)}:=2\sum_{S\in \mathcal{S}_k^{\LeftChoice}} U_N(S),
\qquad k=1,\dots,\frac{N-5}{2},
\]
and
\[
U_N:=\sum_{k=0}^{(N-5)/2} U_N^{(k)}
=
U_N(\emptyset)+2\sum_{k=1}^{(N-5)/2}\sum_{S\in \mathcal{S}_k^{\LeftChoice}} U_N(S).
\]
Then
\[
W_N+d(U_N)=2\sum_{S\in \mathcal{S}_{(N-3)/2}^{\LeftChoice}}V_N(S).
\]
\end{theorem}

By Proposition~\ref{prop:VN-properties}, item~\ref{it:V_low_valence}, every graph on the right-hand side has only $3$- and $4$-valent vertices.

\begin{proof}
By Proposition~\ref{prop:VN-properties}, item~\ref{it:lr_iso},
\[
U_N=\sum_{k=0}^{(N-5)/2}\sum_{S\in \mathcal{S}_k}U_N(S).
\]
By Lemma~\ref{lem:differential-UN},
\[
d(U_N)
=
\sum_{k=0}^{(N-5)/2}
\left(
\sum_{T\in \mathcal{S}_{k+1}}V_N(T)-\sum_{S\in \mathcal{S}_k}V_N(S)
\right)
=
\sum_{S\in \mathcal{S}_{(N-3)/2}}V_N(S)-V_N(\emptyset).
\]
By Proposition~\ref{prop:VN-properties}, item~\ref{it:V_is_wheel}, the graph $V_N(\emptyset)$ is the wheel graph $W_N$. Hence
\[
W_N+d(U_N)=\sum_{S\in \mathcal{S}_{(N-3)/2}}V_N(S).
\]
Again by Proposition~\ref{prop:VN-properties}, item~\ref{it:lr_iso},
\[
\sum_{S\in \mathcal{S}_{(N-3)/2}}V_N(S)
=
2\sum_{S\in \mathcal{S}_{(N-3)/2}^{\LeftChoice}}V_N(S).
\]
This proves the theorem above.
\end{proof}
